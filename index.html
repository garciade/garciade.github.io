About me

Hi! My name is Aitor and I recently graduated at Videogames Programming from Teesside University. During the last year of my studies at ESAT, I formed part of Modular Souls, with whom I developed "Fuel Renegades", which is published on Steam and received a few awards during the students game contest. For my final year's project at Teesside I created "Doomsday Clock", a FPS tower defense in VR, available for both Oculus Rift and HTC Vive. The project was chosen and featured at Expotees '19.

Apart from that I also have experience with networking programming, AI, graphics, mobile, low level and game jams, but what I enjoy most when making games is the gameplay, the things involved in the player-game interactions and making all those feel good.

My main goal is to continue learning, and I believe that being part of the industry is the best way to do it. That's is why I'm willing to join a nice team to help with my passion and sense of humour.

Fuel Renegades

Arcade multiplayer racing game published on Steam.

Fuel Renegades is an arcade multiplayer racing game developed by Modular Souls, a group of nine students (four artists and five programmers) for our final project at ESAT. It was made using Unreal Engine 4 and C++, and got published on Steam.

The main features of the game are its polished mechanics and the smooth game feel. You can have a lot of fun playing versus the AI but where it shines brightest is when played with friends, both on split-screen (up to 4 players) or online (up to 8 players).

My contributions to the project were:

  • Design and implementation of some of the gameplay features such as the trails' system, boost-pads, traps and countdown mini-game.
  • General game design (since we didn't have any proper designers in our team, some of the programmers that were interested in that field united forces and knowledge to fill that role).
  • Most of the particles and in-game feedback.
  • Some UI design and animation of most of it.
  • Iteration and polished of the game.

The game also received some awards during the Student Game Contest ‘18:

  • Best Videogame (Second Place).
  • Best Technology (First Place).

Doomsday Clock

VR tower defense FPS available for Oculus and Vive.

Doomsday Clock is a tower defense first person shooter in virtual reality. It was developed completely by me using Unity and SteamVR, and works for both HTC Vive and Oculus Rift. The game was part of my final year's project at Teesside University, and was chosen to be featured at Expotees '19.

The goal of the game is to last alive as long as possible, keeping the evil cubes at bay with different weapons. The cubes propagates in distinctives ways towards the player, aiming to destroy its fortress.

Some of the project's features are:

  • Custom hands animations.
  • DOTween usage to make the game look cooler.
  • Sound manager that organizes all audios of the game from one place based on singleton's events.
  • Editor tools that creates cubes walls with different parameters to try, debug, and show new stuff easily.
  • Pretty particles, materials, animations, effects and a bunch of details that makes it fun to play.

I must say that the development of the game had several complications and setbacks, and not especially because of the technical difficulty. Developing for virtual reality is fantastic but it has one bad thing: you need a VR development kit. As I did not have my own, I depended on one lent by the university. In the first instance, I wasted valuable time trying to obtain permission to access the VR lab, and once there I had to adapt to the schedules and the availability of the equipment so I was limited and could not work fluently. I would have loved to continue developing the project since I had to discard many ideas and most of the ones I did stayed halfway, but I will not be able to do so until I have my own development kit, which I cannot afford right now.

Fleje World

Lunar lander-like game made for the 38th Ludum Dare Game Jam.

I created Fleje World for my first ever participation on a game jam, the 38th edition of the Ludum Dare. It was made in just 48 hours using Unity. The whole process was a huge experience, and even though this is not my most technical project I learned a lot from it, especially about time management and tasks organization.

The game was inspired by the Atari's arcade "Lunar lander", where the goal is to land a space ship on the surface of different planets. My version of the game is a bit different, on it the only actions the player can make are trigger the propulsors and rotating the ship. There's no time limit to land, but what is limited is the fuel usage. There are six different levels (from the moon to a black hole) and there are a couple variables that change the difficulty on each level like the shape of them and the pull force exerted to the ship.
